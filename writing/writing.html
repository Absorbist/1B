Foundation

Key Success Factors

Three essential principles for writing effective job application emails

️

Professional Language

Using appropriate, professional-sounding language throughout your email. Avoid slang, contractions, and overly casual expressions.

✨

Positive Self-Image

Conveying a positive image of yourself without appearing overconfident or arrogant. Be factual and positive about your skills.

🎯

Avoid Basic Mistakes

Avoiding basic mistakes which will make you look careless. Check spelling, grammar, punctuation, and formatting carefully.

Best Practices

Tips for Writing a Covering Email

Compare notes with a partner and discuss how relevant you think each other's information is, what you should leave out, and what else you might want to include.

01

Open Appropriately

Use appropriate sentences to open the email/letter. State clearly why you are writing.

"I am writing to apply for the post of receptionist advertised on your website."
02

Organize into Paragraphs

Organize the main body into clear paragraphs: qualifications, experience, and suitability.

Paragraph 1: Personal info & skills. Paragraph 2: Experience. Paragraph 3: Why you're suitable.
03

Use Formal Style

Don't use contractions or very informal expressions. Use formal vocabulary where appropriate.

Use "require" instead of "need", "as" instead of "because"
04

Use Conditionals

The use of a conditional can often sound more polite and professional.

"I would welcome the chance to..." instead of "I want to..."
05

Be Factual & Positive

When explaining why you're suitable, be factual and positive, but not overconfident. Avoid sounding arrogant.

"I believe I would be suitable..." not "I am the perfect candidate..."
06

Close Appropriately

Use appropriate phrases to close the email/letter professionally.

"Yours sincerely," (when you know the name) or "Yours faithfully," (when you don't)
⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid
❌ Don't:

Include irrelevant personal information (e.g., "we lost touch when I came home")

Don't:

Mention family connections unprofessionally (e.g., "He is, in fact, my uncle")

❌ Don't:

Use informal language or contractions (e.g., "I'd love to", "I've done")

✅ Do:

Keep content relevant to the job requirements

✅ Do:

Proofread carefully for spelling and grammar

✅ Do:

Use conditional forms for politeness
